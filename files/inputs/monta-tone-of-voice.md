# Monta Tone of Voice Playbook (TOV)

## Introduction

Monta's Tone of Voice (TOV) is how we connect with users ‚Äî across Hub, Charge, Control, and every communication touchpoint.
This guide goes beyond the Content Style Guide by focusing on **why and how we sound the way we do**, helping writers, designers, and product teams consistently create copy that is clear, human, and motivating.

The Style Guide is the **rulebook** (spelling, terminology, formatting).
This TOV Guide is the **playbook** (strategy, application, and examples).

## How Brand Values Shape Tone

Monta's brand values actively shape how we communicate.
They define our consistency across all channels and products, regardless of audience or scenario.
Monta's voice is **human, bold, confident, and inspiring** ‚Äî always approachable, never arrogant.

| Brand Value | Tone Impact | Example (Do / Don't) |
|--------------|-------------|----------------------|
| **Human-Driven Technology** | Approachable and jargon-free ‚Äî people first, systems second. | **Do:** "Charging paused ‚Äî here's what to do next."<br>**Don't:** "OCPP handshake error." |
| **Future-Focused Innovation** | Bold, visionary, forward-looking ‚Äî grounded in reality. | **Do:** "Together, we're building the infrastructure for a cleaner tomorrow."<br>**Don't:** "We will single-handedly revolutionize global energy." |
| **Change Through Collaboration** | Transparent, professional, and collaborative. | **Do:** "Here's how Monta supports your network growth."<br>**Don't:** "Your account settings have been changed." |

## Tone Across the User Journey

Tone is the emotional color of Monta's communication ‚Äî how we sound in each moment.
Writers and designers should adjust tone based on:

- **Journey stage:** awareness, onboarding, everyday use, problem resolution, partner communications
- **Technical literacy:** consumer, operator, admin
- **Context sensitivity:** critical vs. routine tasks

### Tone Across the Journey Matrix

| Journey Stage | User Mindset | Tone Guidance | Examples |
|----------------|---------------|----------------|-----------|
| **Awareness & Exploration** | Curious, evaluating options, low trust | Storytelling-driven, aspirational, energetic ‚Äî grounded in reality. | **Do:** "Discover how Monta helps businesses scale EV infrastructure with ease."<br>**Don't:** "We're the #1 EV platform, no contest." |
| **Onboarding (First-Time Use)** | Excited but cautious | Supportive, clear, and welcoming. Step-based, not overwhelming. | **Do:** "Let's get your first charging station online ‚Äî follow these 3 steps."<br>**Don't:** "Setup required before use." |
| **Everyday Use (Active Customers)** | Task-focused, time-sensitive | Direct, efficient, reassuring. | **Do:** "Station online. Session ready to start."<br>**Don't:** "Device status: operational." |
| **Problem Resolution (Errors & Support)** | Frustrated, anxious | Calm, neutral, proactive. | **Do:** "Payment didn't go through ‚Äî update your card or try again."<br>**Don't:** "Error 104: transaction failed." |
| **Partner & Enterprise Communications** | Strategic, ROI-focused | Professional, collaborative, transparent ‚Äî approachable. | **Do:** "Here's this quarter's feature updates to improve fleet efficiency."<br>**Don't:** "Your account settings have been changed." |

## Quick Reference

- **User literacy:** More context for new users, more precision for technical users.
- **Journey stage:** Inspire early, guide during onboarding, reassure in problem states.
- **Accessibility:** Use plain language, descriptive links, visible form labels, color + text for errors.
- **Consistency:** Use unified terminology (Charger, Charging station, EVSE).
- **Personalization:** Use "you," "your," or names when available.
- **Clarity over hype:** Avoid jargon and overpromising; keep enthusiasm grounded in reality.

## Audience-Specific Guidelines

### EV Drivers
- **Tone:** Friendly, encouraging, consumer-focused
- **Complexity:** Minimal technical detail unless necessary
- **Focus:** Quick wins, immediate actions, troubleshooting
- **Language:** Everyday terms, avoid industry jargon

**Example:** "Finding a charge point is easy with the Monta Charge app. Open the map, tap on any green pin, and check if it's available."

### Site Owners
- **Tone:** Professional, business-oriented, ROI-focused
- **Complexity:** Moderate‚Äîbalance business and technical concepts
- **Focus:** Setup, management, revenue, utilization
- **Language:** Business terms with light technical context

**Example:** "Configure access controls to make your charge points available only to tenants or customers, maximizing utilization while maintaining security."

### Operators
- **Tone:** Technical, professional, comprehensive
- **Complexity:** High‚Äîassume technical knowledge, provide detail
- **Focus:** System configuration, integrations, advanced features
- **Language:** Technical terminology, API references, industry standards

**Example:** "Configure OCPP 1.6-J connectivity by entering your charge point's unique identifier and endpoint URL in the Hardware Portal, then verify the WebSocket connection status."

## Writing Style Guidelines

### Use Active Voice
Active voice is clearer and more direct than passive voice.

**Good:** "The system sends a webhook when the session completes."
**Avoid:** "A webhook is sent by the system when the session is completed."

### Use Second Person ("You")
Address the reader directly to create engagement and clarity.

**Good:** "You can start a charging session from the mobile app."
**Avoid:** "Users can start charging sessions from the mobile app." or "One can start a charging session..."

### Write in Present Tense
Present tense feels more immediate and actionable.

**Good:** "The API returns a 200 status code on success."
**Avoid:** "The API will return a 200 status code on success."

### Keep Sentences and Paragraphs Short
Break complex ideas into digestible pieces. Aim for 15-25 words per sentence.

**Good:**
"Authentication requires two credentials: a client ID and client secret. You'll receive these after completing partner onboarding. Store them securely."

**Avoid:**
"Authentication, which is required for API access, requires two credentials (a client ID and a client secret) that you'll receive after completing the partner onboarding process, and these should be stored securely in your application."

### Use Lists for Steps and Options
Lists improve scannability and comprehension.

**Good:**
```
To start a charging session:
1. Open the Monta Charge app
2. Select your charge point
3. Tap "Start Charging"
4. Wait for confirmation
```

**Avoid:**
"To start a charging session, open the Monta Charge app, select your charge point, tap Start Charging, and wait for confirmation."

### Front-Load Important Information
Put the most important information at the beginning of sentences and paragraphs.

**Good:** "Webhook signatures verify request authenticity. All webhook requests include an X-Signature header."
**Avoid:** "All webhook requests include an X-Signature header, which you can use to verify authenticity."

## Technical Writing Standards

### Code Examples
- Always include realistic, runnable examples
- Use consistent formatting and indentation
- Comment code when the purpose isn't obvious
- Show both request and response for API examples

### API Documentation
- Specify HTTP methods, endpoints, and parameters clearly
- Document all required and optional parameters
- Include authentication requirements
- Provide example requests and responses
- Document error codes and their meanings

### Error Messages and Troubleshooting
- Describe what went wrong in plain language
- Explain why it matters
- Provide clear remediation steps
- Link to related documentation when helpful

**Example:**
```
**Error:** "Charge point not found (404)"

**Cause:** The charge point ID doesn't exist or you don't have permission to access it.

**Solution:**
1. Verify the charge point ID is correct
2. Check that your API credentials have access to this charge point
3. Confirm the charge point is active in the portal
```

### Screenshots and Visuals
- Use screenshots sparingly‚Äîonly when they add value
- Annotate images to highlight key areas
- Keep images up-to-date with current UI
- Provide alt text for accessibility
- See `concepts/images-and-media.md` for detailed guidelines

## Formatting Conventions

### Capitalization
- **Product names:** Monta Hub, Monta Charge, Partner API, Public API
- **Features:** Use sentence case (e.g., "price groups" not "Price Groups")
- **UI elements:** Match the exact capitalization in the interface
- **Headings:** Use sentence case (e.g., "Getting started with the Partner API")

### Links
- Use descriptive link text (not "click here")
- Link to specific sections when possible
- Reference official API docs with full URLs

**Good:** "Learn more about [authentication flows in the Partner API](https://docs.partner-api.monta.com/authentication)."
**Avoid:** "Click [here](https://docs.partner-api.monta.com) for more information."

### Code and UI References
- Use `backticks` for code, API endpoints, parameters, and file names
- Use **bold** for UI elements users should click or interact with
- Use _italics_ sparingly for emphasis

**Example:** "Click **Settings** in the sidebar, then enter your `clientId` in the **API Credentials** section."

### Numbers and Units
- Spell out one through nine; use numerals for 10+
- Use numerals for technical values (e.g., "3 kWh", "2 API calls")
- Include appropriate units (kWh, kW, minutes, seconds)
- Use commas for thousands (e.g., "1,000 charge points")

## Things to Avoid

### ‚ùå Marketing Speak
This is documentation, not a sales pitch.

**Avoid:** "Our revolutionary AI-powered platform delivers unparalleled charging experiences!"
**Use:** "Monta Hub provides tools to manage charge points, pricing, and user access."

### ‚ùå Unnecessary Words
Cut filler words that don't add meaning.

**Avoid:** "In order to start a session, you will need to basically just tap the button."
**Use:** "To start a session, tap the button."

### ‚ùå Assumptions About User Knowledge
Don't assume everyone knows industry terms or has the same context.

**Avoid:** "Configure OCPP as usual."
**Use:** "Configure OCPP 1.6-J connectivity using your charge point's unique identifier."

### ‚ùå Gendered Pronouns
Use "they/their" or rewrite to avoid pronouns.

**Avoid:** "When a user logs in, he can access his charge points."
**Use:** "When users log in, they can access their charge points." or "After logging in, users can access their charge points."

### ‚ùå Emojis and Emoticons
Keep documentation professional.

**Avoid:** "That's it! üéâ You're all set! üòä"
**Use:** "Setup complete. You can now start using the API."

## Quality Checklist

Before publishing documentation, verify:

- [ ] Is the information accurate and up-to-date?
- [ ] Is it written for the right audience level?
- [ ] Are instructions clear and testable?
- [ ] Are all technical terms explained on first use?
- [ ] Are code examples functional and realistic?
- [ ] Are links working and pointing to the right content?
- [ ] Is the formatting consistent with other documentation?
- [ ] Have you removed unnecessary jargon and filler words?
- [ ] Would a new user understand this without additional context?
- [ ] Are images optimized and have alt text?
- [ ] Does the tone match the user's journey stage?
- [ ] Does the copy sound human, bold, confident, and inspiring?
